A COMPARISON BETWEEN DIFFERENT INTERPOLATION METHODS FOR CHARACTER UPPER BODY ANIMATION

by

Xingyu Lei

 

A Thesis

Submitted to the Faculty of Purdue University

In Partial Fulfillment of the Requirements for the degree of

 

Master of Science

Department of Computer Graphics Technology

West Lafayette, Indiana

Fall 2020

 

ABSTRACT

 

Character animation are not only for games or films anymore, today it is being widely used in many other fields, for instance: virtual learning in education and humanoid motion studies in medical or robotics research. Data-driven methods for animating characters including keyframe editing and motion capture are most commonly used but incapable of real-time generation. Dynamic simulation is fully procedural but is far too complex to implement on a full-body scale and offers limited control. Machine learning proves to be successful in creating accurate and robust movement on synthesizing the character’s lower-body, but not for the upper-body.

 

Motion synthesis assembles motion primitives and generates full animation sequence procedurally in real-time. The gap between two motion primitives or keyframes is being filled using different interpolation methods. Interpolation using Euler angles or rotation matrix is intuitive and widely used in animation packages such as Maya. Interpolation using Quaternion is used more in game engines such as Unity due to its fast computation.

 

This thesis compares the naturalness of character animation using three common interpolations: Linear Euler interpolation, Spherical Linear Quaternion interpolation and Spherical Spline Quaternion interpolation. The mathematical equations, motion curves and velocity graphs are well studied. This thesis focuses on the perceptional aspect of evaluating these interpolations on upper-body character animation, as upper-body movements are more expressive. Four types of body gestures with corresponding animation will be recorded using motion capture technology and serve as the ground truth. These recordings will then be processed to create the three different interpolation animation stimuli. The three interpolation clips along with the original motion-captured clip is tested in perceptional experiments which ask participants to evaluate how natural the animation looks using three different test methods: Two-Alternative Forced Choice, Yes/No and Rating.
